Interdisciplinariety is unavoidable

I want to propose to you an exercise: try to come up with one scientific discipline that exists in total isolation of others.

Of course, there is none. One can only consider the epistemic landscape of modern science as a woven fabric of threads that cross each other. In practice, no discipline exists in a total vacuum, and even highly specific research objects are informed by other disciplines either in terms of methodology or the different levels of complexity that inform that object. This should be a self-evident reality. Even in mathematics, arguably the potentially more insular discipline possible, any given subfield is necessarily informed by levels of theoretical complexity that support the tools of mathematical exploration; a theory of numbers depends on a cultural understanding of numbers, for example.

In that sense, it’s more common to do interdisciplinary research than not. Interdisciplinarity is unavoidable, because our reality don’t always align with the neatly defined categories that are departments or scientific disciplines. I sometimes think that entire disciplines aren’t but the success of selling an interdisciplinary enterprise in a neatly presented package – for an extreme case, take any species-specific discipline in Biology, such as Primatology. Primatology is, effectively, only held together by its subject of study, despite a wide range of methodologies, focuses and relationships with other disciplines (Ecology, Ethology, Genetics…).
